5 Clinical "Red Flags" in Pediatric Documentation

An Attorney's Guide to Identifying Nursing Standard of Care Deviations in Complex Litigation.

1. The "Vitals Gap" (Recognition of Decline)

Pediatric patients compensate longer than adults but deteriorate rapidly. Standards of care require frequent, age-appropriate vital sign monitoring.

The Red Flag

"Stable" vitals recorded for hours, followed by a sudden, catastrophic event with no documented "trend" of decline (e.g., progressive tachycardia or tachypnea).

2. Medication "Weight-Based" Errors

Nearly all pediatric dosing is weight-based (mg/kg). A deviation in the recorded weight can lead to a sentinel event.

The Red Flag

Medication administered based on an "estimated" weight rather than a dual-verified actual weight, or a failure to recalculate dosing after a significant weight change.

3. Delayed "Escalation of Care"

Nurses have an independent duty to advocate for the patient. If a provider does not respond, the nurse must "move up the chain of command."

The Red Flag

Documentation shows a nurse paged a physician multiple times with no response, but the nurse failed to escalate to a charge nurse or supervisor.

4. "Nursing Judgment" Failures

Standards require nurses to apply the complete "Nursing Process": Assess, Diagnose, Plan, Implement, and Evaluate.

The Red Flag

Treatment was implemented, but there is no follow-up documentation 30–60 minutes later to evaluate if the patient improved.

5. Flowsheet vs. Narrative Inconsistency

The medical record must be a cohesive clinical narrative.

The Red Flag

Electronic flowsheet shows "Skin WNL" (Within Normal Limits), but the narrative note mentions "redness at IV site" or "bruising noted."
